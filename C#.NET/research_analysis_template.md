## General Analysis Tips

### Reading Strategy:
1. **Start with the abstract** - Get an overview of the entire paper
2. **Read the introduction** - Understand the problem and motivation
3. **Skim the methodology** - Understand the approach without getting lost in details
4. **Focus on results** - Look for key findings and comparisons
5. **Read the conclusion** - Understand the implications and future work

### Critical Thinking Questions:
- Is the problem clearly defined and motivated?
- Are the methods appropriate for addressing the problem?
- Are the results convincing and well-presented?
- Do the conclusions follow logically from the results?
- What are the broader implications of this work?

### Common Limitations to Look For:
- Small sample sizes or limited datasets
- Specific assumptions that may not hold in general
- Computational or resource constraints
- Limited scope of evaluation or testing
- Potential biases in methodology or data

## Student Writing Style Guidelines

When writing your analysis, use:
- **Clear, concise language** - Avoid unnecessary jargon
- **Academic tone** - Professional but accessible
- **Critical thinking** - Question assumptions and methods
- **Evidence-based conclusions** - Support your analysis with specific examples from the paper
- **Personal insights** - Add your own thoughts on the work's significance

### Example Phrases for Student Analysis:
- "The authors propose a novel approach to..."
- "The results demonstrate that..."
- "However, a limitation of this study is..."
- "Future research could explore..."
- "This work contributes to the field by..."
